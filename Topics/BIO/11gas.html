Gas exchange in humans

  • Respiration uses oxygen to oxidise food and so release the energy that cells need to stay alive.
  • Respiration produces carbon dioxide and water vapour as waste products : glucose + oxygen --> energy + carbon dioxide + water
  • Living organisms must be able to take oxygen from the air and get rid of carbon dioxide to the air.
  • Swapping oxygen for carbon dioxide is called gas exchange of gaseous exchange.
  • Gas exchange takes place through a gas exchange surface, also known as respiratory surface.
  • The properties of an ideal respiratory surface are :

    • Property of surface Reason
    thin(ideally one cell thick) gases have a short distance over which to diffuse.
    large surface area many molecules of gas can diffuse across at the same time.
    well ventilated Concentration gradients for oxygen and carbon dioxide are kept up by regular fresh supplies of air.
    close to blood supply Gases can be carried to and from the cells that need or produce them.

  • The human breathing system
    • The Nose & Mouth
      • The nose and mouth are separated by the palate.
      • It is better to breath through nose because the structure of nose allows the air to become warm, moist and filtered before it gets to the lungs.
      • Hairs in the nose trap dust particles in the air.
      • Inside the nose are some thin bones called turbinal bones which are covered with a thin layer of cells. Some of these cells called goblet cells, make a liquid containing water and mucus which evaporates into the air in the nose and moistens it.
      • Other cells have very tiny hair – like projections called cilia. The cilia are always moving and bacteria or particles of dust get trapped in them and in the mucus. Cilia are also found all along the trachea and bronchi
    • Trachea (windpipe)
      • From the nose or mouth, the air then passes into the windpipe or trachea.
      • At the top of the trachea is a piece of cartilage called the epiglottis.
      • Epiglottis closes the trachea and stops food going down the trachea when you swallow as a reflex action that happens automatically when a bolus of food touches the soft palate.
      • Below the epiglottis is the voice box or larynx. This contains the vocal cords.
      • The vocal cords can be tightened by the muscles so that they make sounds when air passes over them.
      • The trachea has rings of cartilage around it which keep it open.
    • Bronchus
      • The trachea divides into two. The two branches are called the right and left bronchi.
      • One bronchus goes to each lung and then branches out into smaller tubes called bronchioles.
    • Alveoli (air sac)
      • At the end of each bronchiole are many tiny sacs or alveoli. This is where gas exchange takes place.
      • Tiny capillaries are closely wrapped around the outside of the alveoli.
      • The walls of the alveoli have several features which make them an efficient gas exchange surface :
      • The capillary walls are only one cell thick.
      • Blood is constantly pumped to the lungs along the pulmonary artery. This branches into thousands of capillaries which take blood to all parts of the lungs.
      • They have large surface area.
      • They have a good supply of oxygen.

Breathing movement

  • Breathing is the set of muscular movement that gives the respiratory surface a constant supply of fresh air.
  • Breathing is brought about by the action of two groups of muscles – the intercostal muscles (between the ribs) and the diaphragm (a large sheet of muscle and elastic tissue underneath the lungs and heart)
  • Breathing in (inspiration)
    • the muscles of the diaphragm contract, the diaphragm is pulled downwards, which increases the volume in the thorax. 
    • the external intercostal muscles contract, the rib cage is pulled upwards and outwards.
    • as the volume of the thorax increases the pressure inside it falls below atmospheric pressure.
    • air therefore rushes in along the trachea and bronchi into the lungs.
  • Breathing out (expiration)
    • the muscles of the diaphragm relax, the diaphragm springs back up into its domed shape and the volume in the thorax decreases.
    • the rig cage drops down again into its normal position and this also decreases the volume in the thorax.
    • A comparison of inspired and expired air

    Component of air Inspired (inhaled) air /% Expired (exhaled) air/% Reason
    Oxygen 21 18 oxygen has diffused from the air in the alveoli into the blood
    Carbon dioxide 0.04 3 carbon dioxide has diffused from the blood into the air in the alveoli
    Nitrogen 78 78 nitrogen gas is not used by the body 
    Water vapour Very variable Saturated water evaporates from surfaces in the alveoli
    Temperature Very variable 37oC heat is lost to the air from the lung surfaces


    Exercise and breathing

    • When we work very hard, our muscles use up a lot of energy. 
    • The heart and lungs, even working flat out cannot supply enough oxygen to provide the energy by aerobic respiration. Muscles can release energy from food without using oxygen by anaerobic respiration which produce lactic acid. [ glucose --> energy + lactic acid ]
    • If lactic acid is built up in the cells, it inhibits muscular contraction which leads to fatigue.
    • The heart, the liver and the kidneys will need extra oxygen to get rid of the lactic acid, provided by the deep fast breathing that follows hard exercise. This extra oxygen is called oxygen debt
